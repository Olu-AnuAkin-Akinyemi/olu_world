# **Part 1: Build the Foundation First**

Before any campaign or pitch, lock in these four things. They are the infrastructure everything else runs on.

## **1\. Sync-Ready Assets**

This is the most overlooked step. Before you pitch a single agency, every relevant track needs:

* Lossless WAV file (44.1kHz / 24-bit minimum)

* Instrumental version (broadcast quality — not just vocals removed)

* TV Mix — vocals drop slightly, no abrupt ending, loops or fades cleanly

* Stems available — drums, bass, lead vocal, synths as separate stereo files. Supervisors sometimes remix to fit scenes. This is increasingly expected, not optional.

* Metadata embedded in every file — ISRC code, BPM, key, mood tags, genre, your name as both artist and composer

* Clear split sheet documenting who wrote what percentage. If you used a sample, clear it before pitching anything.

* Short intros. Supervisors skip tracks that take too long to get going. If a track has a long intro, create an alternate version that gets to the vocal faster.

*Music supervisors will not chase you for proper files. If your track is not “one-stop” — meaning fully cleared, instantly licensable — it gets skipped, no matter how good it is.*

## **2\. Your Electronic Press Kit (EPK)**

Your EPK is a one-page professional introduction. It should exist as both a PDF and a live webpage. It tells supervisors, journalists, and curators what they need in under 90 seconds.

* Short bio — 3 sentences max. Who you are, what your sound is, why it matters now

* Artist photo — your motion-blur / shadow photography already works perfectly

* Streaming links — Spotify, Apple Music, Bandcamp

* Sync-specific language: “one-stop cleared,” moods and genres your catalog fits

* Your logo — use the geometric triangle mark

* Contact info — direct email only. No contact form.

## **3\. Your Website as a Sync Portfolio**

Your website should work on two levels at once — fan-facing and industry-facing. Your current site already does this better than most artists in your lane (see the reference analysis). Key pages:

* Home — cinematic visual world, first impression like a film title card

* Afterglow — project page with mood descriptions per track (supervisors search by mood)

* Sync Licensing — industry-facing section. One-stop info, download EPK. Clean and factual.

* Visual Reels — 30-45 second clips of your music with visuals. Supervisors often watch before they listen.

* Contact — direct email only. Never a form.

## **4\. Register Everything**

Before you license anything, your rights must be registered. No exceptions.

* ASCAP or BMI — join one as songwriter/publisher. Collects performance royalties when your music plays on TV or radio.

* SoundExchange — collects digital performance royalties from streaming and internet radio.

* Create a publishing entity — even something simple like “AnuAkin Music.” Register it with your PRO.

* Copyright registration — file each track with the US Copyright Office at copyright.gov. Costs $35-65, legally protects you in disputes.

# **Part 2: Promotion — Unique Campaigns**

These are not generic “post more content” suggestions. Each campaign is built for an artist in your lane — cinematic, identity-driven, visually sophisticated. Research from Music Ally’s 2025 Campaigns of the Year report confirms: world-building, mystery campaigns, and cinematic storytelling are the top-performing marketing trends heading into 2026\.

## **Campaign 1: The Afterglow Micro-Film**

Your most powerful single move. Instead of three separate music videos, combine Afterglow tracks into one 2-3 minute short film. A visual tone poem — not a traditional narrative, not a lyric video.

* Use your existing motion-blur and shadow photography as the visual language

* Structure following the Afterglow emotional arc: Magnetic Electric (attraction / the pull) → WTFYA (arrival / presence) → INDECISION (uncertainty / the question) → ALWYS LØVE(D) (devotion / warmth). The full EP includes an interlude and a closing track — the micro-film distills the arc, it does not need to include every track.

* No dialogue. Just music, movement, color, and texture

* Shoot on location in a single day — intentional cinematography, not a large budget

* Upload to YouTube unlisted first for sync pitches and press

* Then release publicly as a major release moment

*This micro-film does two things at once: promotional piece for fans AND a living demo reel for sync supervisors who want to see your music working with visuals before they call you.*

This is also your “Anchor” — one heavy-lift asset that gets carved into dozens of smaller pieces. Cut 15-30 second clips for Reels, TikToks, and story posts. Pull still frames for feed posts. Use behind-the-scenes footage as separate content. One strong asset becomes a full month of material.

## **Campaign 2: “Scene From a Film That Doesn’t Exist” Series**

A recurring series on Instagram and TikTok. Each post is a 30-60 second cinematic clip — a fragment of a scene from an imaginary film — with one of your tracks underneath.

* Never explain the scene. No caption. Just: a title card, the clip, your music.

* Use found footage, creative commons film clips, or phone footage with intentional framing

* Title each post like a real film: “Scene 04 — Midnight Arrival” or “Scene 11 — The Walk”

* Over time, this builds a visual archive that proves your music works in film language

*This gets attention from two audiences at once: fans who love the aesthetic AND music supervisors who scroll social media looking for artists whose sound already fits visual media. A\&Rs are now actively scouting TikTok and Instagram for sync-ready artists. Multiple sync discoveries have started exactly this way.*

## **Campaign 4: The Filmmaker Outreach Campaign**

Bypass the music industry entirely. Go directly to the people who need music.

Independent filmmakers, short film directors, and student film programs constantly need music but cannot afford major licenses. This is your in.

* Find 20-30 independent short film directors on Vimeo and Instagram whose visual aesthetic matches yours — dark, moody, character-driven

## **Campaign 5: The Limited Physical Object Drop**

Release something physical that cannot be streamed. Genuine scarcity in an era of instant availability.

* USB card — credit-card sized, matte black, logo embossed. Pre-loaded with Afterglow in lossless, the micro-film, and a visual PDF lookbook. 50-100 units.

* Hand-numbered zine — session photography alongside lyrics printed as poetry. Sold through Bandcamp and at shows.

* Cassette — your aesthetic is warm and slightly analog. A limited cassette run signals artistic intention. National Audio Company handles small runs.

Send 5-10 directly to music supervisors and sync agency A\&R contacts. A physical object is nearly impossible to throw away. The rest go to press and fans via a drop announcement.

## **Campaign 6: The Cinematic Collaborator Network**

Build a tribe of visual artists who speak the same aesthetic language.

* Find 3-5 photographers, videographers, fashion designers, or visual artists whose work matches your dark / mystical / cinematic quality

* Propose mutual projects — you provide music for their portfolio shoots, they provide visual content for your releases

* Share each other’s work on social. Cross-pollinate audiences. The best collaborations happen between artists with overlapping but not identical audiences.

* Create a shared visual identity language — when their work and your music appear together repeatedly, it builds a recognizable world that press and supervisors notice

## **Campaign 7: The Mystery / Ritual Campaign (New for 2026\)**

Music Ally’s research shows mystery-driven campaigns are outperforming standard release marketing. Cryptic teasers, hidden messages, and participatory elements raise emotional stakes and deepen fan investment. For your aesthetic, this is native territory.

* Before a release, post a series of cryptic visuals — fragments of collage work, close-up textures, coordinates, a date — with no explanation

* Use your email list to send coded messages or private links before anything goes public. Reward the inner circle.

* Create a hidden page on your website (not in the nav) with unreleased content. Share the link only through stories that disappear in 24 hours.

* The goal: make people feel like they discovered something, not that they were sold something.

## **The Afterglow Palette Playlist — Detailed Guide**

This is not a throwaway Spotify playlist. It is a strategic tool that does three things at once: positions you alongside established artists in algorithm space, keeps listeners on your profile longer (which Spotify tracks), and gives curators and supervisors a ready-made mood board of your sonic world. Here is exactly how to build it.

### **What to Call It**

Name it something evocative and specific to your world — not generic. “The Afterglow Palette” works because it ties directly to your project and sounds like a curated experience, not a random collection. Avoid names like “My Favorites” or “Chill Vibes.” The title should make someone curious enough to click before they know what’s inside.

### **How to Structure It**

The playlist should be 25-40 tracks, roughly 90 minutes to 2 hours. This is the sweet spot: long enough that Spotify’s algorithm treats it as a substantial playlist, short enough that it feels curated rather than dumped.

**Track placement matters.** Put one of your tracks in positions 1-3 (the opening sets the tone and gets the most plays). Then space your other tracks roughly every 5-7 songs throughout. Don’t cluster all your music together at the top — that signals self-promotion. Interleaving signals confidence: your music belongs alongside these artists, it doesn’t need special treatment.

A suggested structure for Afterglow Palette:

* Tracks 1-3: Open with one of yours (WTFYA or Magnetic Electric), then two established artists that set the mood

* Tracks 4-10: Deep cuts from your reference artists. Not their biggest hits — the tracks that actually match your sound.

* Track 8 or 9: Your second track

* Tracks 11-20: Broaden slightly — artists adjacent to your lane that listeners might not know yet

* Track 16 or 17: Your third track

* Tracks 21-30+: Mix of mood-matched songs that complete the emotional arc. End with something reflective — ALWYS LØVE(D) works perfectly as a closer.

### **Which Artists to Include**

The artists on this playlist are not random favorites. They are strategic signals to Spotify’s algorithm about who your listeners overlap with. Every artist you include teaches the algorithm to recommend you to their fans, and them to yours.

**Core references (your direct lane):** Sampha, Obongjayar, serpentwithfeet, Blood Orange, Moses Sumney, Kelela, Sade, Solange

**Adjacent artists (expand the circle):** Frank Ocean, Jon Batiste, James Blake, FKA twigs, Jorja Smith, Daniel Caesar, Kadhja Bonet, Mereba

**Deep cuts / discovery:** Duval Timothy, Nala Sinephro, MIKE, Navy Blue, Loyle Carner, Joy Crookes, Barney Artist

*The algorithm learns from co-occurrence. When listeners who save Sampha tracks also save your tracks on the same playlist, Spotify starts connecting those dots. Over time, your music shows up in “Fans Also Like” for these artists and in Discover Weekly for their listeners. This is free, passive discovery that compounds over months.*

### **The Playlist Description**

Write the description in your scene language, not generic playlist copy. Something like:

*Cinematic soul for the space between midnight and morning. Devotional, layered, warm. The sonic world around Afterglow by ØLu AnuAkin.*

Include your artist name in the description — this helps with search. Keep it under 300 characters. No hashtags. No “follow me.” Let the curation speak.

### **How to Maintain It**

* Update the playlist every 2-4 weeks. Swap in 3-5 new tracks, remove a few older ones. Spotify’s algorithm favors active playlists over stale ones.

* When you release new music, put the new track in position 1-2 of this playlist immediately. Existing followers get exposed to it automatically.

* Pin this playlist to the top of your Spotify artist profile. It should be the first thing someone sees when they visit your page.

* Share it everywhere: your website, your Instagram bio (through Songlink), your EPK, your sync pitches. It doubles as a mood reference for anyone trying to understand your sound in 90 seconds.

### **Why This Matters for the Algorithm in 2026**

Spotify’s algorithm now heavily favors retention signals over raw stream counts. Saves, repeat listens, and playlist adds carry far more weight than a one-time play. A well-built artist playlist directly feeds these signals: listeners save it, return to it, and play your tracks in context alongside artists they already love. That repeat-listen context is exactly what triggers Discover Weekly and Daily Mix placements.

One more thing: Spotify’s NLP system scans playlist titles, descriptions, and blog posts to understand how your music is categorized. A playlist called “The Afterglow Palette” described as “cinematic soul” trains the algorithm to categorize you correctly — which means better recommendations to the right listeners, not random ones.

# **Part 4: Sync Licensing — How to Actually Get In**

Most artists get this wrong. They send emails. They submit to every form they find. They wait. Nothing happens. Here’s why — and what actually works.

## **Understand the Hierarchy**

| Who | What They Do | How to Reach Them |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Music Supervisor** | Chooses which songs go in specific scenes. Works directly with directors. | Hard to reach directly as an unknown. Best reached through sync agencies who already have their trust. |
| **Sync Agency** | Represents artists. Clears rights. Pitches your music to supervisors on your behalf. Takes 20-30% commission. | Your primary target. Get signed with a boutique agency and they open supervisor relationships for you. |
| **Sync Library** | Searchable catalog of pre-cleared music. Supervisors browse and license directly. | Easier to get into. Apply directly. Builds placement history that makes agency pitches stronger. |

*Important distinction (from Berklee): a sync rep only takes commission when they get you a placement. A publishing company takes some of your publishing earnings. If someone calling themselves a sync rep tries to take your publishing, that’s a red flag.*

## **Your Sync Strategy: Three Lanes**

Run all three simultaneously. Don’t put all effort into one path.

**Lane 1 — Boutique Sync Agencies:** Pitch your full cinematic package to 5-8 selective agencies. Personalize every pitch.

**Lane 2 — Sync Libraries:** Submit to 3-5 curated platforms that accept indie artists. Smaller but consistent placement income, builds track record.

**Lane 3 — Direct Filmmaker Network:** Campaign 4 above. Build credits independently, outside the commercial sync world.

## **Boutique Agencies to Target**

| Agency | Why It Fits | How to Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Crucial Music** | Non-exclusive deals. 50/50 split. No upfront fees. Personal pitching. Strong indie roster. 3-year terms but you can request removal. | Direct email. Include EPK \+ micro-film link. Describe tracks in mood/scene terms. |
| **Secret Road** | Boutique. Artist-friendly. Strong TV supervisor relationships. Non-exclusive. Champions indie artists. | Email submission. Emphasize visual aesthetic. They respond to identity and world-building. |

| **A\&G Sync (UK)** | Boutique UK agency. Netflix and BBC placement history. Cinematic artists fit well. | Email with EPK. UK agencies are less saturated with US indie pitches — strategic advantage. |

## **Sync Libraries to Submit To**

These accept independent submissions, don’t require agency representation, and pay you directly. Build your catalog here while pursuing agency relationships.

* Musicbed — high-end curated. Used by filmmakers, ad agencies, branded content. Strong emotional arcs preferred.

* Music Vine — UK-based. Widely used in film/TV. Accepts cinematic/alternative artists. Non-exclusive available.

* Artlist — large platform, millions of video creators. Pays upfront for annual licensing. Consistent catalog income. Non-exclusive.

# **Part 5: How to Write Your Sync Pitch**

Your pitch email is a 30-second introduction. Supervisors receive dozens per week. The goal is not to impress them with your story. The goal is to make it fast and easy for them to say yes.

## **The Pitch Formula**

**Subject line — specific, not mysterious:** ØLu AnuAkin — Alternative Soul / Indie R\&B Sync Pitch | Afterglow (One-Stop Cleared)

The email body — short, structured, scene-oriented:

Hello \[Name\],

I create Alternative Soul / Indie R\&B — emotionally layered, cinematic music with hip-hop influences. Sharing three tracks from Afterglow, a cohesive sound palette designed around stylized visual moments.

WTFYA — Confident arrival energy. Scene: character stepping into the room and owning it.

Magnetic Electric — Warm romantic tension. Scene: two people circling each other, golden-hour light.

ALWS LØVE(D) — Reflective, still, unresolved. Scene: end of something, sunset, alone with thoughts.

All tracks one-stop cleared. Stems, instrumentals, and TV mixes available on request.

Visual reel: \[micro-film link\]  |  Streaming: \[playlist link\]

Thank you for your time. — ØLu AnuAkin

What this email does NOT contain: your life story, stream counts, social followers, flattery, or “just give it a listen.” It describes scenes, not just feelings — supervisors think in scenes.

### **Where to Host Your Pitch Links**

Don’t attach MP3 files. Don’t send a long list of links. Send a single streaming link where they can quickly preview. Best options:

* Private SoundCloud playlist (clean, easy to play)

* Disco.ac link (industry standard for sync pitching, built for this)

* Box.com or Google Drive folder (simple, no account needed to listen)

## **Follow-Up Protocol**

If nothing after 2-3 weeks, send one short follow-up. If still nothing — move on. Add new music, build credits, pitch again with your next project. Persistence means consistently doing good work, not repeatedly emailing the same person.

# **Part 6: Your 90-Day Action Timeline (Budget-Phased)**

The original timeline assumed everything starts at once. The reality is that legal infrastructure and promotion run on different budgets and different timelines. This version is sequenced by what you can do now with no money, what costs under $300, and what requires a larger budget. The promotion starts immediately. The sync infrastructure catches up when funds allow.

*The most expensive mistake is waiting to promote until the legal/financial side is perfect. Your visual world and your music already exist. Every week you don’t put them in front of people is a week of lost momentum that costs nothing to reclaim except your time and creative energy.*

## **Phase 1: Start Now ($0 — This Week)**

Everything here is free. No excuses, no dependencies. This is the foundation that every later phase builds on.

1. Register with SoundExchange (soundexchange.com). Free. Takes 15 minutes. Collects digital performance royalties retroactively for a limited period — every day you wait is potential money lost.

2. Resolve ASCAP / BMI signup. Call ASCAP member services directly (1-800-952-7227) or try BMI instead (free for songwriters, $150 one-time for publishers). Do not let a website glitch cost you royalty collection. If one platform gives you trouble, use the other.

3. Claim your Spotify for Artists and Apple Music for Artists profiles if you haven’t already. Upload artist photo, bio in scene language, and your Afterglow cover art.

4. Build The Afterglow Palette playlist on Spotify (see the detailed guide in Part 3). Pin it to the top of your Spotify profile.

5. Start the “Scene From a Film That Doesn’t Exist” series on Instagram and TikTok. You have the photography, the aesthetic, and the music. Shoot the first 3-5 posts this week using your phone with intentional framing.

6. Set up Songlink / Odesli for each track (free). Use these smart links in every social post and story instead of raw Spotify or Bandcamp URLs.

7. Update your website bio and any social bios with the exact genre language: Alternative Soul / Indie R\&B — emotionally layered, cinematic, with hip-hop influences.

8. Begin reaching out to 5-10 independent filmmakers on Vimeo and Instagram whose visual style matches yours. Offer a track for their next project in exchange for credit and a clip for your reel.

## **Phase 2: First Budget Drop ($150-300 — Weeks 2-4)**

This is where you spend money on things that protect your work and open doors that free effort alone cannot.

1. Register all Afterglow tracks with the US Copyright Office (copyright.gov). \~$35-65 per track, roughly $140-260 for the EP. This is not optional — it is the legal foundation everything else rests on. Without it, no serious sync agency will touch you.

2. Create your EPK as a PDF. You already have every asset you need from the website: artist photo (motion-blur shot), bio, streaming links, logo, track descriptions in scene language. Assemble it into a clean one-page PDF. Cost: $0 if you use Canva free tier or design it yourself.

3. Launch a small Groover campaign ($20-40). Submit 10-20 curators targeting cinematic, Alt Soul, and Indie R\&B playlists. Use scene language in your pitch notes: “Fits the moment a character stands at a crossroads, 2am, city quiet.”

4. Submit to Spotify editorial via Spotify for Artists at least 7-10 days before your next release. This is free and one of the highest-leverage actions available to any indie artist.

5. Continue the Scene series at 3-5 posts per week. By now you should have 10-15 posts building the visual archive.

6. Begin planning the Afterglow micro-film. Scout a location. Identify one collaborator (photographer or videographer) willing to trade their time for your music on their reel. The shoot itself can cost $0 if you work with mutual-exchange collaborators.

## **Phase 3: Sync Preparation ($300-600 — Weeks 5-8)**

Now you start building the assets that make sync agencies say yes. This is where you save and allocate for stems and mixes.

1. Budget and commission mixing and sync assets. The actual costs are phased:

   * Phase 1 mixing ($400): Two new Afterglow tracks mixed and finalized — do this first
   * Phase 1.5 mastering ($250–500): Master all Afterglow tracks
   * Phase 2 mixing ($1,157.16): Instrumentals, TV mixes, stems for all tracks — required before sync pitching begins, not before site launch or promotion

   Prioritize the tracks with the strongest scenes: WTFYA (arrival/power), Magnetic Electric (romance/tension), ALWYS LØVE(D) (reflection/ending). You don’t need every track sync-ready on day one — start with the strongest three.

2. For each track, produce: instrumental version, TV mix (vocals lowered, clean fade), and separated stems (drums, bass, vocal, synths/keys). If budget is tight, prioritize instrumentals first — they’re requested most often. Stems can follow.

3. Embed full metadata in every file: ISRC code, BPM, key, mood tags, genre, your full name as artist and composer. Use a tool like Kid3 (free) or your DAW’s metadata editor.

4. Release the Afterglow micro-film. Post the full film as a major moment on YouTube and socials. Upload an unlisted version for sync pitches and press.

5. Submit to 2-3 sync libraries that accept indie artists without an agency: Musicbed, Music Vine, Artlist. These build your track record and generate small but consistent income while you pursue agency relationships.

6. Form your publishing entity. An LLC costs $50-150 depending on your state. Name it (e.g., AnuAkin Music LLC). Register it with ASCAP or BMI as your publisher. This lets you collect the publisher’s share of performance royalties — without it, you’re leaving 50% of your sync royalties uncollected.

## **Phase 4: Agency Pitches and Expansion ($50-200 — Weeks 9-12)**

By now you have: copyright registration, sync-ready assets for at least 3 tracks, an EPK, a visual reel (the micro-film), a growing social presence, and possibly a few indie filmmaker credits. You are ready to pitch agencies.

1. Compile your boutique agency pitch list: Crucial Music, Secret Road, Marmoset, Position Music, A\&G Sync. Research each one’s current roster and recent placements before writing your pitch.

2. Personalize and send your pitch package to each agency, spaced 3-5 days apart. Use the pitch formula from Part 5\. Every email gets a unique opening line that references something specific about that agency.

3. Activate the Sync Licensing page on your website (currently commented out in your index.html). Populate it with the one-stop cleared language, mood tags, and a link to download your EPK.
